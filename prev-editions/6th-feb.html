Let's look at some of the most common concerns I saw this week in the community.

"I've learnt some HTML, CSS and JS, built a few projects in React. Do I need to learn Data Structures, BigO and other CS stuff?"

It can be pretty useful! Swizec, who was a guest on Scrimba pod, wrote an absolute belter of an article on how he figured out a slow part in his app, measured it, and fixed it! The measuring and fixing part required some knowledge of CS fundamentals.

"What do I need to learn to be 100% job-ready? Is HTML, CSS, JS enough?"

Yes and no. It all depends on the job. That is the usual answer you'd hear, but that is simply because the real answer is way more in-depth than that. The reality is that no coding school will prepare you for every possible job you could accept. The best way is to learn on the job. This is why this essay "Learn on the Battlefield" by Coding Horror, written in 2007, reads so timelessly. Read the article and apply for some jobs.

"Jobs are pretty hard to get. This shouldn't be that hard."

Or a variation on the topic. Substitute 'getting jobs' with 'my organisation', 'my town', 'my country', 'life' ğŸ˜œ. A lot of things aren't the way they are supposed to be. Cindy writes about how understanding your company helps you align incentives and be the force for change and improvement. "Get comfortable with the 'mess'" and "solve problems, get things done and create change". The essay is so good, that advice can then be projected into other aspects of your life. Truly transcending piece of writing.
